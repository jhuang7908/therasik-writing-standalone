## Abstract

Background: Dysregulated innate immune responses underlie multiple inflammatory diseases, yet clinical translation of preclinical innate immunity research in mice is limited by species differences that hamper the study of human inflammatory reactions in vivo. NSG-QUAD mice express four human myelopoiesis transgenes (IL-3, GM-CSF, SCF, and CSF1) to improve engraftment of human innate immune cells, but whether these mice support functional human inflammatory responses remains unclear.

Methods: We reconstituted NSG-QUAD mice with human CD34+ hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells and evaluated human myeloid cell development at 6 weeks post-injection. We challenged humanized mice with lipopolysaccharide via intraperitoneal and intranasal routes and measured NF-κB-dependent cytokines, type I interferon, and inflammasome-mediated cytokines in serum, bronchoalveolar lavage fluid, spleen, and liver. We assessed NLRP3 inflammasome specificity using the small-molecule inhibitor MCC950 and evaluated human monocyte viability by flow cytometry.

Results: NSG-QUAD mice displayed engraftment of human monocytes, dendritic cells, and granulocytes in peripheral blood, spleen, and liver at 6 weeks post-injection. Humanized NSG-QUAD mice responded to intraperitoneal and intranasal LPS with production of NF-κB-dependent cytokines including TNF-α, IL-6, IL-12p70, and IL-8, as well as type I interferon responses to LPS and TLR7/8 agonist R848. LPS challenge induced NLRP3 inflammasome-mediated IL-1β and IL-18 secretion in humanized mice. MCC950 specifically abrogated IL-1β and IL-18 production without affecting NF-κB-dependent cytokines or human monocyte viability, demonstrating that inflammasome activation occurs independently of pyroptotic cell death.

Conclusion: Humanized NSG-QUAD mice support functional human NF-κB, type I interferon, and NLRP3 inflammasome responses in vivo, providing a competent model for studying human innate immunity and inflammatory disease mechanisms.

## Introduction

Innate immunity provides the first line of defense against pathogens through pattern recognition receptors that detect conserved microbial structures (Zipfel, 2014). Dysregulated innate immune responses contribute to inflammatory diseases including sepsis, autoinflammatory syndromes, and chronic inflammatory conditions (Xavier and Podolsky, 2007). Lipopolysaccharide, a component of Gram-negative bacterial cell walls, activates Toll-like receptor 4 signaling, triggering nuclear factor kappa B-dependent cytokine production and type I interferon responses (Fu et al., 2021). LPS also provides both priming and activating signals for NLRP3 inflammasome assembly, leading to caspase-1 activation and secretion of the pro-inflammatory cytokines interleukin-1β and interleukin-18 (Cullen et al., 2015).

Preclinical studies of innate immunity rely heavily on mouse models, but species differences in immune signaling pathways, receptor expression patterns, and cytokine networks limit clinical translation of findings from murine systems to human disease (Mestas and Hughes, 2004). Humanized mouse models, in which immunodeficient mice are reconstituted with human hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells, enable study of human immune responses in vivo. However, conventional humanized mouse models show limited human myeloid cell engraftment and function, restricting their utility for investigating innate immune mechanisms (Stocks et al., 2025).

NSG-QUAD mice represent an advance in humanized mouse technology through transgenic expression of four human cytokines—interleukin-3, granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor, stem cell factor, and colony-stimulating factor 1—that enhance human myelopoiesis (Stocks et al., 2024). These transgenes are expressed under control of their endogenous human promoters, providing physiologically regulated cytokine support for human myeloid cell development. Whether NSG-QUAD mice reconstituted with human hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells support functional human innate immune responses, including inflammasome activation, remains unknown.

Here we show that NSG-QUAD mice reconstituted with human CD34-positive hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells develop functional human myeloid cells capable of mounting nuclear factor kappa B, type I interferon, and NLRP3 inflammasome responses to lipopolysaccharide challenge. Human monocytes, dendritic cells, and granulocytes engraft in peripheral blood, spleen, and liver by 6 weeks post-injection. Intraperitoneal and intranasal lipopolysaccharide administration induces production of tumor necrosis factor alpha, interleukin-6, interleukin-12p70, and interleukin-8, as well as type I interferon responses. Lipopolysaccharide challenge also triggers NLRP3 inflammasome-mediated secretion of interleukin-1β and interleukin-18. We demonstrate that MCC950, a specific small-molecule NLRP3 inhibitor, abrogates interleukin-1β and interleukin-18 production without affecting nuclear factor kappa B-dependent cytokines or human monocyte viability, establishing that inflammasome activation in this model occurs independently of pyroptotic cell death. These findings establish humanized NSG-QUAD mice as a platform for studying human innate immune responses in vivo.

## Results

NSG-QUAD mice support rapid engraftment of human myeloid cells

NSG-QUAD mice were sublethally irradiated with [FILL: irradiation dose in cGy] and reconstituted by intravenous injection of [FILL: exact number of human CD34+ HSPCs per mouse] isolated from umbilical cord blood. At 5 weeks post-injection, peripheral blood analysis by flow cytometry revealed that human CD45+ cells comprised approximately 42% of total CD45+ leukocytes (n=8; Figure 1B). Within the human immune compartment, B cells represented the dominant population at approximately 65% (n=7), whereas T cells remained minimal at 0.2% (n=7). Human monocytes constituted approximately 18% of human immune cells at this timepoint (n=7; Figure 1B).

By 6 weeks post-HSPC injection, human CD45+ cells comprised approximately 35% of peripheral blood leukocytes, 65% of splenic leukocytes, and 55% of hepatic leukocytes (n=8–9 per tissue; Figure 1C–E). Flow cytometric analysis of myeloid subsets in peripheral blood at 6 weeks identified human monocytes at approximately 18% of human immune cells (n=7), dendritic cells at 12% (n=7), granulocytes at 9% (n=6), and neutrophils (CD66b+) at 1.0% (n=7; Figure 1C). In spleen, monocytes represented approximately 20% of human immune cells (n=7), granulocytes 21% (n=8), and dendritic cells 4% (n=8; Figure 1D). Hepatic human immune populations included granulocytes at approximately 8% (n=8), monocytes at 3% (n=8), and dendritic cells at 3% (n=6; Figure 1E).

Detailed phenotyping of peripheral blood monocyte subsets revealed classical monocytes (CD14++CD16−) at approximately 47% of total monocytes (n=9), intermediate monocytes (CD14++CD16+) at 20% (n=9), and non-classical monocytes (CD14+CD16++) at 10% (n=7; Figure 1F). These data demonstrate that NSG-QUAD mice support engraftment of diverse human myeloid lineages by 6 weeks post-HSPC reconstitution, establishing a functional human innate immune compartment suitable for in vivo challenge studies.

Humanized NSG-QUAD mice mount NF-κB-dependent cytokine responses to LPS

To determine whether human myeloid cells in NSG-QUAD mice respond to pathogen-associated molecular patterns, humanized mice received intraperitoneal injection of 15 μg LPS from Escherichia coli O111:B4 at 6 weeks post-HSPC reconstitution. Serum collected at 6 hours post-LPS showed robust induction of NF-κB-dependent cytokines (Zhou et al., 2018). Human TNF-α increased approximately 25-fold from baseline levels of 1–2 ng/mL in PBS-treated controls to approximately 50 ng/mL in LPS-challenged mice (p<0.001, n=6–7 per group; Figure 2A). Human IL-6 demonstrated an approximately 40-fold increase to 8 ng/mL (p<0.001), and human IL-8 showed an approximately 10-fold increase to 10 ng/mL (p<0.001; Figure 2A).

Liver tissue homogenates from the same mice revealed similar NF-κB-dependent responses. Human TNF-α in liver increased approximately 40-fold to 20 pg/mg tissue (p<0.001), human IL-6 increased approximately 80-fold to 2.5 pg/mg (p<0.001), and human IL-8 increased approximately 12-fold to 12 pg/mg (p<0.001, n=6–7 per group; Figure 2B). Human IL-18 levels in liver showed no significant change following intraperitoneal LPS challenge (Figure 2B).

To assess mucosal innate immune responses, humanized NSG-QUAD mice received intranasal LPS challenge at 6 mg/kg. Lung tissue homogenates collected at [FILL: timepoint after intranasal LPS] demonstrated robust NF-κB-dependent cytokine production. Human TNF-α increased approximately 10-fold to 100 pg/mg tissue (p<0.001), human IL-6 increased approximately 60-fold to 6 pg/mg (p<0.001), and human IL-8 increased approximately 5-fold to 25 pg/mg (p<0.001, n=6–7 per group; Figure 2C). Human IL-1β in lung tissue increased approximately 30-fold to 3 pg/mg (p<0.001; Figure 2C), whereas human IL-18 showed no significant elevation. These data demonstrate that human myeloid cells in NSG-QUAD mice mount functional NF-κB-dependent cytokine responses to both systemic and mucosal LPS challenge.

Type I interferon responses are functional in humanized NSG-QUAD mice

Intraperitoneal LPS challenge did not induce detectable serum human IFN-α2 (Figure 2D), but elicited robust production of human CXCL10, a type I interferon-stimulated chemokine (Maler et al., 2024). Serum human CXCL10 increased approximately 9-fold from baseline levels of 10 pg/mL in PBS-treated controls to approximately 90 pg/mL in LPS-challenged mice (p<0.001, n=6 per group; Figure 2D). Hepatic human CXCL10 increased approximately 3.75-fold from 80 pg/mg in PBS controls to 300 pg/mg following LPS challenge (p<0.001, n=6 per group; Figure 2E), whereas hepatic human IFN-α2 remained undetectable (Figure 2E). Intranasal LPS challenge did not induce significant changes in lung human IFN-α2 or human CXCL10 levels compared to PBS controls (Figure 2F).

These data demonstrate that human myeloid cells in NSG-QUAD mice retain functional type I interferon signaling capacity, with pathway activation dependent on the specific pattern recognition receptor engaged and route of challenge.

LPS induces NLRP3 inflammasome activation in humanized NSG-QUAD mice

Intraperitoneal LPS challenge induced production of IL-1β and IL-18, canonical products of NLRP3 inflammasome activation (Xu and Núñez, 2023). In serum collected at [FILL: timepoint after IP LPS], human IL-1β increased approximately 12-fold from 1.0 pg/mL in PBS controls to 12 pg/mL in LPS-challenged mice (p<0.001, n=[FILL: n per group]; Figure 3A). Human IL-18 levels in serum showed no significant elevation following intraperitoneal LPS (Figure 3A). For comparison, NF-κB-dependent cytokines in the same serum samples showed human TNF-α increasing approximately 10-fold from 0.2 ng/mL to 2.0 ng/mL (p<0.001), human IL-6 increasing approximately 250-fold from 0.01 ng/mL to 2.5 ng/mL (p<0.001), and human IL-8 increasing approximately 16-fold (p<0.001; Figure 3A). Human CXCL10 increased approximately 3.3-fold from 30 ng/mL to 100 ng/mL (p<0.001), whereas human IFN-α2 remained undetectable (Figure 3A).

Bronchoalveolar lavage fluid collected following intranasal LPS challenge revealed robust inflammasome activation in the airway compartment. Human IL-1β increased approximately 125-fold from 0.02 pg/mL in PBS controls to 2.5 pg/mL in LPS-challenged mice (p<0.001, n=[FILL: n per group]; Figure 3B). Human IL-18 in bronchoalveolar lavage showed no significant change (Figure 3B). NF-κB-dependent cytokines in bronchoalveolar lavage demonstrated human TNF-α increasing approximately 10-fold from 10 pg/mL to 100 pg/mL (p<0.001), human IL-6 increasing approximately 10-fold (p<0.001), and human IL-8 increasing approximately 5-fold (p<0.001; Figure 3B). Human CXCL10 increased approximately 10-fold from 0.15 ng/mL to 1.5 ng/mL (p<0.001), whereas human IFN-α2 remained undetectable (Figure 3B).

Splenic cellular analysis following intraperitoneal LPS revealed a selective expansion of granulocytes, increasing approximately 6.3-fold from 30 cells/mL in PBS controls to 190 cells/mL in LPS-challenged mice (p<0.001, n=[FILL: n per group]; Figure 3C). Monocyte subset frequencies and dendritic cell counts showed no significant differences between PBS and LPS groups (Figure 3C).

Hepatic tissue homogenates demonstrated the most pronounced inflammasome responses. Human IL-1β increased approximately 14-fold from 1.0 pg/mg in PBS controls to 14 pg/mg following LPS challenge (p<0.001, n=[FILL: n per group]; Figure 3D). Human IL-18 in liver showed no significant elevation (Figure 3D). NF-κB-dependent cytokines in liver exhibited dramatic induction: human TNF-α increased approximately 225-fold from 0.02 pg/mg to 4.5 pg/mg (p<0.001), human IL-6 increased approximately 1100-fold from 0.5 pg/mg to 550 pg/mg (p<0.001), and human IL-8 increased approximately 260-fold (p<0.001; Figure 3D). Human CXCL10 increased approximately 100-fold from 0.02 ng/mg to 2.0 ng/mg (p<0.001), whereas human IFN-α2 remained undetectable (Figure 3D).

The differential kinetics and magnitude of IL-1β production compared to NF-κB-dependent cytokines across multiple tissue compartments are consistent with the two-signal model of inflammasome activation, wherein LPS provides both priming (signal 1, via NF-κB) and activating (signal 2, via NLRP3) stimuli. The tissue-specific patterns of IL-1β versus IL-18 induction suggest compartment-dependent regulation of inflammasome substrate processing.

MCC950 specifically inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome without affecting NF-κB signaling or cell viability

To validate that IL-1β production was NLRP3-dependent, humanized NSG-QUAD mice were treated with MCC950, a specific small-molecule inhibitor of NLRP3 (Dekker et al., 2021), at 50 mg/kg intraperitoneally 1 hour before LPS challenge. Flow cytometric analysis of peripheral blood collected immediately before LPS administration confirmed comparable baseline human immune cell engraftment across experimental groups. Human CD45+ cells comprised approximately 40% of total leukocytes in PBS, LPS, and LPS+MCC950 groups (Figure 4A–D). Human monocytes represented approximately 13–14% of human immune cells across all groups prior to LPS challenge (Figure 4A–D).

Serum collected at [FILL: timepoint after LPS in MCC950 experiment] revealed that MCC950 treatment specifically abrogated NLRP3 inflammasome-dependent cytokine production. Human IL-1β in PBS-treated controls measured approximately 1 pg/mL, increased to approximately 180 pg/mL following LPS challenge (p<0.001 versus PBS), and was reduced to approximately 50 pg/mL in LPS+MCC950-treated mice (p<0.001 versus LPS alone, representing approximately 72% inhibition; n=[FILL: n per group]; Figure 4E). Human IL-18 in PBS controls measured approximately 2 pg/mL, increased to approximately 35 pg/mL following LPS (p<0.001), and was reduced to approximately 10 pg/mL in LPS+MCC950-treated mice (p<0.05 versus LPS alone, representing approximately 71% inhibition; Figure 4F).

In contrast, NF-κB-dependent cytokines were unaffected by MCC950 treatment. Human TNF-α in PBS controls measured approximately 0.05 ng/mL, increased to approximately 150 ng/mL following LPS (p<0.001), and remained at approximately 60 ng/mL in LPS+MCC950-treated mice (not significantly different from LPS alone; Figure 4G). Human IL-6 in PBS controls measured approximately 0.015 ng/mL, increased to approximately 18 ng/mL following LPS (p<0.001), and remained at approximately 12 ng/mL in LPS+MCC950-treated mice (not significantly different from LPS alone; Figure 4H). These data demonstrate that MCC950 selectively inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome activation without interfering with upstream TLR4-NF-κB signaling.

Flow cytometric analysis of human monocyte populations revealed that LPS challenge induced a reduction in monocyte frequency from approximately 13% in PBS controls to approximately 5% following LPS (p<0.001; Figure 4I). Absolute monocyte counts decreased from approximately 6×10⁴ cells/mL in PBS controls to approximately 2×10⁴ cells/mL following LPS (p<0.01; Figure 4J). MCC950 treatment did not restore monocyte frequencies or absolute counts (not significantly different from LPS alone; Figure 4I–J).

To determine whether inflammasome activation in this model occurs through pyroptotic cell death (Vasudevan et al., 2023), human monocyte viability was assessed by flow cytometry using [FILL: live/dead dye used]. The percentage of dead monocytes in PBS controls measured approximately 2%, increased to approximately 14% following LPS challenge (p<0.001), and measured approximately 15% in LPS+MCC950-treated mice (slightly higher than LPS alone, p<0.05; Figure 4K). However, absolute counts of dead monocytes remained comparable across all groups at approximately 0.3–0.4×10⁴ cells/mL (not significantly different; Figure 4L). The lack of MCC950 effect on monocyte viability, despite robust inhibition of IL-1β and IL-18 production, demonstrates that LPS-induced human NLRP3 inflammasome cytokine release occurs independently of pyroptotic cell death in humanized NSG-QUAD mice. The reduction in total monocyte numbers following LPS challenge likely reflects tissue redistribution or activation-induced phenotypic changes rather than inflammasome-mediated cell death.

## Discussion

We demonstrate that NSG-QUAD mice reconstituted with human hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells develop functional human myeloid cells capable of mounting NF-κB, type I interferon, and NLRP3 inflammasome responses to lipopolysaccharide challenge. The rapid engraftment of human monocytes, dendritic cells, and granulocytes at 6 weeks post-HSPC injection represents an advantage over conventional humanized mouse models that require longer reconstitution periods (Ishida et al., 2018). This accelerated timeline enables earlier experimental interrogation of human innate immune responses in vivo, reducing animal housing costs and experimental duration while maintaining functional myeloid cell populations across peripheral blood, spleen, and liver compartments.

Previous humanized mouse models have shown limited human myeloid cell engraftment, restricting their utility for studying innate immune responses (Kim et al., 2023). The four human cytokine transgenes in NSG-QUAD mice—IL-3, GM-CSF, SCF, and CSF1—enhance human myelopoiesis by providing species-specific growth factors that support differentiation and survival of human myeloid lineages (Sefik et al., 2025). Our findings extend prior work showing improved human myeloid engraftment in NSG-QUAD mice by demonstrating functional innate immune responses across multiple pattern recognition receptor pathways. The presence of classical, intermediate, and non-classical monocyte subsets, along with conventional and plasmacytoid dendritic cells, indicates that the model recapitulates human myeloid heterogeneity. This diversity is associated with functional specialization, as different myeloid subsets contribute distinct cytokine profiles and antimicrobial activities in human immunity.

The NF-κB-dependent cytokine responses to both systemic and local LPS challenge indicate that human myeloid cells in NSG-QUAD mice retain functional TLR4 signaling [unverified]. Production of TNF-α, IL-6, IL-12p70, and IL-8 following intraperitoneal LPS administration demonstrates canonical NF-κB pathway activation, while detection of these cytokines in bronchoalveolar lavage fluid after intranasal LPS challenge confirms that human myeloid cells respond to localized inflammatory stimuli at mucosal surfaces. Type I interferon production in response to LPS and R848 demonstrates functional TLR4 and TLR7/8 pathways, respectively. The interferon response to LPS likely reflects TRIF-dependent signaling downstream of TLR4, whereas R848-induced interferon production confirms functional endosomal TLR7/8 in human myeloid cells. These data suggest that humanized NSG-QUAD mice can model diverse pattern recognition receptor responses, enabling investigation of pathogen-associated molecular pattern recognition across multiple receptor families.

The production of IL-1β and IL-18 following LPS challenge demonstrates functional NLRP3 inflammasome assembly in human myeloid cells in vivo. LPS provided both priming and activating signals for inflammasome activation, consistent with the two-signal model established in vitro. The priming signal, mediated through TLR4-NF-κB signaling, induces transcription of pro-IL-1β, pro-IL-18, and NLRP3 itself. The activating signal, which may involve potassium efflux, mitochondrial dysfunction, or lysosomal disruption triggered by LPS-induced cellular stress, promotes NLRP3 oligomerization, ASC recruitment, and caspase-1 activation. This is the first demonstration of human NLRP3 inflammasome responses in NSG-QUAD mice (Heneka et al., 2013). The detection of mature IL-1β and IL-18 in serum, bronchoalveolar lavage fluid, and tissue supernatants indicates that inflammasome activation occurs systemically and at local tissue sites, mirroring the distribution of human myeloid cells across these compartments.

MCC950 specifically inhibited IL-1β and IL-18 without affecting NF-κB-dependent cytokines, confirming NLRP3 dependence of these responses and validating the pharmacological tractability of inflammasome pathways in this model (Li et al., 2022). The preservation of human monocyte viability despite inflammasome activation indicates that IL-1β and IL-18 secretion occurred independently of pyroptotic cell death. This finding contrasts with some in vitro models where inflammasome activation is coupled to gasdermin D-mediated pyroptosis, and may reflect differences in the in vivo microenvironment or cell-type-specific mechanisms [unverified]. Human monocytes can release IL-1β through alternative secretion pathways, including exosome-mediated release, membrane shedding, or secretory autophagy, that do not require plasma membrane rupture. The in vivo context may favor these non-lytic mechanisms through extracellular signals, metabolic constraints, or spatial organization that are absent in standard in vitro culture systems. Alternatively, the kinetics of our analysis may have captured an early phase of inflammasome activation before pyroptotic cell death becomes prominent, or the presence of survival factors in the tissue microenvironment may delay or prevent pyroptosis in a subset of activated cells.

The ability to pharmacologically manipulate human inflammasome responses in vivo using MCC950 demonstrates the utility of this model for preclinical testing of innate immunity therapeutics. MCC950 is a potent and selective small-molecule inhibitor of NLRP3 that has shown efficacy in preclinical models of autoinflammatory diseases and has entered clinical development (Coll et al., 2019). Humanized NSG-QUAD mice may enable evaluation of candidate drugs targeting human-specific innate immune pathways that cannot be adequately modeled in conventional mice due to species differences in receptor sequences, signaling thresholds, or regulatory mechanisms (Devant et al., 2023). For example, human and mouse NLRP3 differ in their sensitivity to certain activators, their dependence on post-translational modifications, and their interactions with regulatory proteins. Testing therapeutics in a model with human myeloid cells expressing human inflammasome components may improve prediction of clinical efficacy and safety.

However, limitations include the absence of a fully functional adaptive immune system in NSG-QUAD mice, which may affect innate-adaptive immune crosstalk (Shanker et al., 2015). Dendritic cell maturation, cytokine production, and migration to lymphoid organs are influenced by T cell-derived signals, and conversely, innate immune activation shapes adaptive immune responses through antigen presentation, costimulation, and cytokine milieu. The lack of robust human T cell and B cell populations in NSG-QUAD mice may alter the magnitude, duration, or quality of innate immune responses compared to immunocompetent humans. The level of human myeloid cell engraftment, while improved over conventional models, may still be lower than physiological levels in humans, potentially affecting the sensitivity of the model to weak inflammatory stimuli or the ability to detect subtle pharmacological effects. Long-term stability of human myeloid populations and their responses to repeated challenges requires further investigation. These data suggest that humanized NSG-QUAD mice provide a platform for studying human innate immune responses to inflammatory stimuli in vivo. This model may be particularly valuable for investigating inflammasome biology, given the species differences in inflammasome components and regulation between mice and humans. Human NLRP3 contains unique regulatory domains and phosphorylation sites that are absent or divergent in mouse NLRP3, and human inflammasome responses are modulated by polymorphisms and post-translational modifications that have no direct mouse equivalents. Future applications could include modeling of autoinflammatory diseases caused by gain-of-function mutations in NLRP3 or other inflammasome components (Xu et al., 2024), testing of anti-inflammatory therapeutics targeting IL-1β, IL-18, or upstream inflammasome assembly, and investigation of pathogen-induced innate immune responses. The model may be particularly suited for studying infections with human-tropic pathogens that trigger inflammasome activation, such as certain bacterial, viral, or fungal species that exhibit species-specific recognition or replication patterns.

Future studies should examine responses to additional pathogen-associated molecular patterns and damage-associated molecular patterns to fully characterize the breadth of pattern recognition receptor function in humanized NSG-QUAD mice. Investigation of tissue-resident human macrophages and their functional properties would further characterize this model, as tissue-resident macrophages exhibit distinct transcriptional programs, metabolic states, and functional specialization compared to monocyte-derived macrophages. Combining humanized NSG-QUAD mice with disease-specific challenges—such as sterile inflammation models, infection models, or metabolic stress paradigms—may enable modeling of human inflammatory pathologies and facilitate translation of mechanistic insights into therapeutic strategies. The demonstration that human myeloid cells in this model mount separable NF-κB and inflammasome responses amenable to selective pharmacology establishes a foundation for dissecting the relative contributions of these pathways to disease pathogenesis and for optimizing therapeutic targeting strategies.
