DEVELOPMENT OF HUMAN INNATE IMMUNE RESPONSES IN HUMANIZED NSG-QUAD MICE
================================================================================


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ABSTRACT  [262 words]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Background: Dysregulated innate immune responses underlie multiple inflammatory diseases, yet clinical translation of preclinical innate immunity research in mice is limited by species differences that hamper the study of human inflammatory reactions in vivo. NSG-QUAD mice express four human myelopoiesis transgenes (IL-3, GM-CSF, SCF, and CSF1) to improve engraftment of human innate immune cells, but whether these mice support functional human inflammatory responses remains unclear.

Methods: We reconstituted NSG-QUAD mice with human CD34+ hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells and evaluated human myeloid cell development at 6 weeks post-injection. We challenged humanized mice with lipopolysaccharide via intraperitoneal and intranasal routes and measured NF-κB-dependent cytokines, type I interferon, and inflammasome-mediated cytokines in serum, bronchoalveolar lavage fluid, spleen, and liver. We assessed NLRP3 inflammasome specificity using the small-molecule inhibitor MCC950 and evaluated human monocyte viability by flow cytometry.

Results: NSG-QUAD mice displayed engraftment of human monocytes, dendritic cells, and granulocytes in peripheral blood, spleen, and liver at 6 weeks post-injection. Humanized NSG-QUAD mice responded to intraperitoneal and intranasal LPS with production of NF-κB-dependent cytokines including TNF-α, IL-6, IL-12p70, and IL-8, as well as type I interferon responses to LPS and TLR7/8 agonist R848. LPS challenge induced NLRP3 inflammasome-mediated IL-1β and IL-18 secretion in humanized mice. MCC950 specifically abrogated IL-1β and IL-18 production without affecting NF-κB-dependent cytokines or human monocyte viability, demonstrating that inflammasome activation occurs independently of pyroptotic cell death.

Conclusion: Humanized NSG-QUAD mice support functional human NF-κB, type I interferon, and NLRP3 inflammasome responses in vivo, providing a competent model for studying human innate immunity and inflammatory disease mechanisms.

INTRODUCTION  [421 words]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Innate immune cells are the first responders to infection and tissue damage, detecting conserved microbial and danger signals through pattern-recognition receptor families and initiating downstream signaling cascades that shape both local and systemic inflammation. Among the best-characterized of these are NF-κB-dependent transcription, which drives pro-inflammatory cytokine gene expression; type I interferon signaling, which coordinates antiviral and immunomodulatory programs; and NLRP3 inflammasome activation, which mediates post-translational processing of interleukin-1β and interleukin-18 [CITE: NLRP3 inflammasome]. Dysregulation of these pathways underlies sepsis, autoinflammatory syndromes, inflammatory bowel disease, and a growing spectrum of complex disorders [CITE: innate immunity inflammatory disease].

Despite extensive mechanistic work in mouse models, translating preclinical innate immunity research into effective human therapies has proven consistently difficult. The innate immune system exhibits striking species differences: human and murine Toll-like receptor repertoires, cytokine networks, and inflammasome compositions diverge at multiple levels, meaning that conclusions drawn from mouse experiments do not reliably predict human biology or clinical drug responses [CITE: mouse human immune differences]. Studying human-specific innate immune mechanisms therefore requires experimental systems that cannot be replicated in standard inbred mouse strains.

Humanized mouse models, generated by engrafting human hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells (HSPCs) into immunodeficient hosts, offer a partial solution. NOD-scid-Il2rγ−/− (NSG) mice support efficient HSPC engraftment and have enabled advances in human adaptive immunology; however, the absence of cross-reactive human myeloid growth factor signaling results in poor reconstitution of human monocytes, dendritic cells, and granulocytes in standard NSG mice [CITE: humanized mouse NSG myeloid]. This myeloid deficiency severely restricts the utility of conventional humanized mouse platforms for innate immunity research.

Transgenic expression of human myeloid growth factors can rescue this limitation. NSG-SGM3 mice, expressing human SCF, GM-CSF, and IL-3, markedly improve granulocyte and monocyte reconstitution, while NSG-CSF1 mice, expressing human CSF1, selectively enhance macrophage and monocyte development. NSG-QUAD mice combine all four transgenes—IL-3, GM-CSF, SCF, and CSF1—creating a host environment that simultaneously supports multiple branches of human myelopoiesis [CITE: NSG-QUAD transgenic myeloid]. Whether this expanded myeloid compartment translates into functional innate immune competence in vivo has not been systematically established.

Here we report that humanized NSG-QUAD mice reconstituted with human HSPCs develop a diversified human myeloid compartment across blood, spleen, and liver by 6 weeks post-engraftment and mount coordinated NF-κB, type I interferon, and NLRP3 inflammasome responses to both systemic and mucosal LPS challenge. Using the selective NLRP3 inhibitor MCC950, we demonstrate pathway-specific pharmacological modulation, establishing humanized NSG-QUAD mice as a tractable in vivo platform for studying human innate immunity and evaluating therapeutics that target these pathways.

DISCUSSION  [1301 words]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The central finding of this study is that humanized NSG-QUAD mice mount coordinated, pharmacologically separable human innate immune responses in vivo—encompassing NF-κB-dependent cytokine production, type I interferon signaling, and NLRP3 inflammasome activation—across both systemic and mucosal compartments. The rapid engraftment of human monocytes, dendritic cells, and granulocytes at 6 weeks post-HSPC injection represents an advantage over conventional humanized mouse models that require longer reconstitution periods (Ishida et al., 2018). This accelerated timeline enables earlier experimental interrogation of human innate immune responses in vivo, reducing animal housing costs and experimental duration while maintaining functional myeloid cell populations across peripheral blood, spleen, and liver compartments.

Previous humanized mouse models have shown limited human myeloid cell engraftment, restricting their utility for studying innate immune responses (Kim et al., 2023). The four human cytokine transgenes in NSG-QUAD mice—IL-3, GM-CSF, SCF, and CSF1—enhance human myelopoiesis by providing species-specific growth factors that support differentiation and survival of human myeloid lineages (Sefik et al., 2025). Our findings extend prior work showing improved human myeloid engraftment in NSG-QUAD mice by demonstrating functional innate immune responses across multiple pattern recognition receptor pathways. The presence of classical, intermediate, and non-classical monocyte subsets, along with conventional and plasmacytoid dendritic cells, indicates that the model recapitulates human myeloid heterogeneity. This diversity is associated with functional specialization, as different myeloid subsets contribute distinct cytokine profiles and antimicrobial activities in human immunity.

The NF-κB-dependent cytokine responses to both systemic and local LPS challenge indicate that human myeloid cells in NSG-QUAD mice retain functional TLR4 signaling [unverified]. Production of TNF-α, IL-6, IL-12p70, and IL-8 following intraperitoneal LPS administration demonstrates canonical NF-κB pathway activation, while detection of these cytokines in bronchoalveolar lavage fluid after intranasal LPS challenge confirms that human myeloid cells respond to localized inflammatory stimuli at mucosal surfaces. Type I interferon production in response to LPS and R848 demonstrates functional TLR4 and TLR7/8 pathways, respectively. The interferon response to LPS likely reflects TRIF-dependent signaling downstream of TLR4, whereas R848-induced interferon production confirms functional endosomal TLR7/8 in human myeloid cells. These data suggest that humanized NSG-QUAD mice can model diverse pattern recognition receptor responses, enabling investigation of pathogen-associated molecular pattern recognition across multiple receptor families.

The production of IL-1β and IL-18 following LPS challenge demonstrates functional NLRP3 inflammasome assembly in human myeloid cells in vivo. LPS provided both priming and activating signals for inflammasome activation, consistent with the two-signal model established in vitro. The priming signal, mediated through TLR4-NF-κB signaling, induces transcription of pro-IL-1β, pro-IL-18, and NLRP3 itself. The activating signal, which may involve potassium efflux, mitochondrial dysfunction, or lysosomal disruption triggered by LPS-induced cellular stress, promotes NLRP3 oligomerization, ASC recruitment, and caspase-1 activation. This is the first demonstration of human NLRP3 inflammasome responses in NSG-QUAD mice (Heneka et al., 2013). The detection of mature IL-1β and IL-18 in serum, bronchoalveolar lavage fluid, and tissue supernatants indicates that inflammasome activation occurs systemically and at local tissue sites, mirroring the distribution of human myeloid cells across these compartments.

MCC950 specifically inhibited IL-1β and IL-18 without affecting NF-κB-dependent cytokines, confirming NLRP3 dependence of these responses and validating the pharmacological tractability of inflammasome pathways in this model (Li et al., 2022). The preservation of human monocyte viability despite inflammasome activation indicates that IL-1β and IL-18 secretion occurred independently of pyroptotic cell death. This finding contrasts with some in vitro models where inflammasome activation is coupled to gasdermin D-mediated pyroptosis, and may reflect differences in the in vivo microenvironment or cell-type-specific mechanisms [unverified]. Human monocytes can release IL-1β through alternative secretion pathways, including exosome-mediated release, membrane shedding, or secretory autophagy, that do not require plasma membrane rupture. The in vivo context may favor these non-lytic mechanisms through extracellular signals, metabolic constraints, or spatial organization that are absent in standard in vitro culture systems. Alternatively, the kinetics of our analysis may have captured an early phase of inflammasome activation before pyroptotic cell death becomes prominent, or the presence of survival factors in the tissue microenvironment may delay or prevent pyroptosis in a subset of activated cells.

The ability to pharmacologically manipulate human inflammasome responses in vivo using MCC950 demonstrates the utility of this model for preclinical testing of innate immunity therapeutics. MCC950 is a potent and selective small-molecule inhibitor of NLRP3 that has shown efficacy in preclinical models of autoinflammatory diseases and has entered clinical development (Coll et al., 2019). Humanized NSG-QUAD mice may enable evaluation of candidate drugs targeting human-specific innate immune pathways that cannot be adequately modeled in conventional mice due to species differences in receptor sequences, signaling thresholds, or regulatory mechanisms (Devant et al., 2023). For example, human and mouse NLRP3 differ in their sensitivity to certain activators, their dependence on post-translational modifications, and their interactions with regulatory proteins. Testing therapeutics in a model with human myeloid cells expressing human inflammasome components may improve prediction of clinical efficacy and safety.

However, limitations include the absence of a fully functional adaptive immune system in NSG-QUAD mice, which may affect innate-adaptive immune crosstalk (Shanker et al., 2015). Dendritic cell maturation, cytokine production, and migration to lymphoid organs are influenced by T cell-derived signals, and conversely, innate immune activation shapes adaptive immune responses through antigen presentation, costimulation, and cytokine milieu. The lack of robust human T cell and B cell populations in NSG-QUAD mice may alter the magnitude, duration, or quality of innate immune responses compared to immunocompetent humans. The level of human myeloid cell engraftment, while improved over conventional models, may still be lower than physiological levels in humans, potentially affecting the sensitivity of the model to weak inflammatory stimuli or the ability to detect subtle pharmacological effects. Long-term stability of human myeloid populations and their responses to repeated challenges requires further investigation. These data suggest that humanized NSG-QUAD mice provide a platform for studying human innate immune responses to inflammatory stimuli in vivo. This model may be particularly valuable for investigating inflammasome biology, given the species differences in inflammasome components and regulation between mice and humans. Human NLRP3 contains unique regulatory domains and phosphorylation sites that are absent or divergent in mouse NLRP3, and human inflammasome responses are modulated by polymorphisms and post-translational modifications that have no direct mouse equivalents. Future applications could include modeling of autoinflammatory diseases caused by gain-of-function mutations in NLRP3 or other inflammasome components (Xu et al., 2024), testing of anti-inflammatory therapeutics targeting IL-1β, IL-18, or upstream inflammasome assembly, and investigation of pathogen-induced innate immune responses. The model may be particularly suited for studying infections with human-tropic pathogens that trigger inflammasome activation, such as certain bacterial, viral, or fungal species that exhibit species-specific recognition or replication patterns.

Future studies should examine responses to additional pathogen-associated molecular patterns and damage-associated molecular patterns to fully characterize the breadth of pattern recognition receptor function in humanized NSG-QUAD mice. Investigation of tissue-resident human macrophages and their functional properties would further characterize this model, as tissue-resident macrophages exhibit distinct transcriptional programs, metabolic states, and functional specialization compared to monocyte-derived macrophages. Combining humanized NSG-QUAD mice with disease-specific challenges—such as sterile inflammation models, infection models, or metabolic stress paradigms—may enable modeling of human inflammatory pathologies and facilitate translation of mechanistic insights into therapeutic strategies. The demonstration that human myeloid cells in this model mount separable NF-κB and inflammasome responses amenable to selective pharmacology establishes a foundation for dissecting the relative contributions of these pathways to disease pathogenesis and for optimizing therapeutic targeting strategies.
